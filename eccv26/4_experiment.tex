\textbf{Device.} 
Our MaST models are implemented using Python 3.10 and PyTorch 2.4.1. Training is conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU. We evaluate inference speed on three representative edge platforms: Raspberry Pi 5, Apple M4, and NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. 
To ensure a consistent evaluation environment and better reflect real-world application deployment, we export ONNX files with OpSet 17 and benchmark with ONNXRuntime 1.20.1 to report practical on-device performance. For trackers whose source code is publicly available, we re-evaluate their speed under the same protocol to ensure a fair comparison.


\textbf{Model.} 
To demonstrate our method on the low-cost edge device, we use ViT-Tiny~\cite{vit} as the encoder and the training are initialized with the MAE-lite~\cite{wang2023maelite}.  
% We provide the 30\% tokens retraining model as MaST-tiny, and a lighter model that only using first 8 layers as MaST-nano, to show the compatibility of our method with layer pruning.  More results with various token retention rates can be seen in ablation studies.   





\textbf{Training.} 
The training splits of COCO~\cite{coco}, LaSOT~\cite{lasot}, GOT-10k~\cite{got10k} and TrackingNet~\cite{trackingnet} are used for training. Common data augmentations including horizontal flip and brightness jittering are used in training. The GPU holds 128 image pairs as the batch size. We train the model with AdamW optimizer~\cite{adamw}, set the weight decay to $10^{-4}$, the initial learning rate for the backbone to $4\times10^{-5}$ and other parameters to $4\times10^{-4}$, respectively. The total training epochs are set to 300 with 60k image pairs per epoch and we decrease the learning rate by a factor of 10 after 240 epochs.  The sparsification rate is gradually warmed-up during training following OSTrack~\cite{ostrack}.  More details of training and hyper-parameters can be found in the supplementary material.


\subsection{Comparison with State-of-the-arts}
We conduct a comprehensive comparison on five widely used tracking benchmarks. Trackers are grouped into three tiers: $\sim$500M, $\sim$1G, and $\sim$2G MACs---based on their computational cost, and compared within the same tier.

\textbf{LaSOT.} LaSOT~\cite{lasot} is a large-scale long-term tracking benchmark comprising 1,400 video sequences with 280 sequences reserved for testing. As shown in~\tabref{tab-sota-efficient}, MaST-tiny achieves the highest AUC of 63.3 and $P_{Norm}$ of 72.2 in the $\sim$1G group, outperforming AsymTrack-S~\cite{zhu2025asymtrack} by +0.5 AUC and HiT-Small~\cite{HiT} by +2.8 AUC, while running at 22.6 FPS on Raspberry Pi 5. MaST-small further pushes to 65.8 AUC in the $\sim$2G group, surpassing FERMT~\cite{fermt} by +0.7 AUC.

\textbf{TrackingNet.} TrackingNet~\cite{trackingnet} is a large-scale short-term benchmark with 511 test sequences covering diverse real-world scenarios. MaST-tiny leads the $\sim$1G group with 80.1 SUC and $P_{Norm}$ of 85.3, surpassing AsymTrack-S by +2.2 SUC. In the $\sim$2G group, MaST-small achieves 82.3 SUC, outperforming all competing methods in its tier.

\textbf{GOT-10k.} GOT-10k~\cite{got10k} is a large-scale dataset with 180 test sequences featuring non-overlapping object categories between train and test splits, designed to measure generalization. MaST-tiny attains an AO of 66.6 and $\text{SR}_{0.75}$ of 62.5 in the $\sim$1G group, outperforming AsymTrack-S by +1.1 AO and +3.6 $\text{SR}_{0.75}$. MaST-small achieves the top AO of 70.0 and $\text{SR}_{0.75}$ of 67.0 in the $\sim$2G group.

% \textbf{UAV Benchmarks.} UAV123~\cite{uav123} and DTB70~\cite{dtb70} are aerial tracking datasets focusing on low-altitude scenarios with fast motion, small targets, and frequent occlusion. As reported in~\tabref{tab:uav-uav123} and~\tabref{tab:uav-dtb70}, MaST-tiny achieves 63.8 AUC on UAV123 and 63.3 AUC on DTB70, outperforming other lightweight trackers such as HiT-Tiny and FEAR-XS. MaST-nano achieves competitive results with 61.3 AUC on UAV123 and 61.8 AUC on DTB70, demonstrating its suitability for resource-constrained aerial tracking tasks.




\subsection{Ablation Study}
% For efficient experiments, We train our model on GOT-10k train split and test on val split for ablation studies as default setting.


\textbf{Impact of Different Sparsification Strategies.}
\tabref{tab:sparse} compares the performance and efficiency of various token sparsification strategies. The dense baseline (``None'') retains all tokens at 1752M encoder MACs, while naive attention-score-only pruning at the first layer (``Attention'') reduces computation to 824M MACs ($2.11{\times}$ speedup) but suffers a sharp \textbf{3.8 AUC} drop---demonstrating that early cross-attention scores alone are too diffuse for reliable pruning.  Replacing the score source with the prediction-box prior (``Prediction'') is worse, dropping to 59.4 AUC.  
The proposed \textbf{Motion Window (MW) mechanism} is the key enabler: combining attention scores with the Gaussian spatial prior (``Attention + Motion'') recovers 3.3 AUC relative to the attention-only baseline while keeping the same 824M MACs and $2.08{\times}$ FPS boost, closing the gap to only \textbf{0.5 AUC} below the dense model.  Using the motion window alone (``Motion'') yields a middle ground, confirming that the fusion of both signals is optimal.  These results underscore that injecting a lightweight temporal motion prior is critical to making aggressive early-layer sparsification practically viable.

\textbf{Analysis of Different Prediction Heads.}
\tabref{tab:head} compares five prediction heads under ViT-Tiny with a dense backbone versus 30\% sparse backbone plus motion window. Two key findings emerge. \textbf{First}, sparsification consistently reduces MACs for every head---by $1.8{\times}$--$1.9{\times}$---with only marginal AUC drops ($\le$1.5 points), confirming that the backbone is the dominant bottleneck. \textbf{Second}, not all heads benefit equally in terms of \emph{speed}: Loc-Token and Transformer-Decoder heads introduce heavy sparse-to-dense reshaping or cross-attention, limiting practical FPS gains. Our 1$\times$1 Conv head is natively sparse, achieving the highest FPS of 48.3 on Raspberry Pi 5 while matching the MLP head in AUC (63.8 dense / 63.8 sparse). We hypothesize that position-dependent designs (boxes are decoded with anchor point) suit lightweight models better than global aggregators (Loc Tokens, Transformer Decoder), where model capacity is insufficient to learn purely attention-driven localization.

\textbf{Impact of Token Retention Rate.}
\figref{token_rate} examines how the token retention rate affects the AUC–FPS trade-off at two
input resolutions on LaSOT.
At 384 resolution, retaining only 30–40\% of tokens already matches the dense baseline in AUC
while substantially boosting throughput; further reduction to 20\% causes only a moderate drop.
At 256 resolution, 50–60\% retention achieves near-lossless accuracy, and 30\% retention remains
competitive.
In both settings the motion-aware prior guides early token selection reliably regardless of input
scale, confirming that MaST's accuracy–efficiency trade-off is robust across resolution choices.

\textbf{Layer-wise Sparsification Analysis.}
\tabref{tab:layer} evaluates the effect of applying token sparsification at different layers (all with motion window) of the ViT-Tiny encoder. {Layer 1} achieves the best balance: 63.8 AUC, 24.6 FPS on Raspberry Pi 5, and the lowest cost of 862M FLOPs. 
Delaying sparsification to later layers yields marginal AUC gains ($\le$0.4 points) at the cost of nearly halving throughput (24.6 $\to$ 12.5 FPS). This confirms that very early pruning guided by the motion window is sufficient and optimal, and we set Layer 1 as default.

\textbf{Why the Sparse Head Enables Additional Speedup.}
\tabref{tab:speed} isolates the contribution of the prediction head to the overall speedup. Dense-compatible heads (e.g., 3$\times$3 conv, Loc Tokens, Transformer Decoder) must first reshape the sparse token set back to a full spatial grid before computation, so their head MACs are \emph{unchanged} by backbone sparsification. In contrast, our 1$\times$1 conv head and the MLP head operate natively on the retained tokens, reducing head MACs by ${\approx}3.4\times$ proportionally to the retention rate. This co-design of a sparse backbone with a natively sparse head is critical: it translates the $2.08\times$ backbone FLOPs reduction into a $\mathbf{2.08\times}$ overall FPS gain on edge devices.

\textbf{Comparison with Alternative Compression Strategies.}
\tabref{tab:compress} compares MaST against two common compression baselines under an identical compute budget ($\approx$850M MACs): (1) \emph{Resolution Reduction}, which downscales the search image from $224$ to $160$ pixels, and (2) \emph{Layer Pruning}, which removes the last 4 Transformer layers. At the same MACs, resolution reduction suffers a 4.2 AUC drop, and layer pruning drops 2.5 AUC, while our token sparsification retains 63.8 AUC---only 0.5 below the dense baseline. Furthermore, our method achieves 48.3 FPS, substantially faster than both baselines, demonstrating that spatial token sparsification is a superior compression strategy for tracking compared to reducing input resolution or model depth.
